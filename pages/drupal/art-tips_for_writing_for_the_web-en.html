Tips for Writing for the Web

Writing for the Web is not the same as writing for a publication because people do not read on the Web the same way they read from paper. In fact, reading from a computer screen takes more time than reading from paper. Reading complicated information on a mobile device is even more difficult because of the small screen. Also, readers of Web content scan rather than read a text.
Consequently, the key to keep readers on your web pages is to write shorter content and to focus on essential content. Here are some tips, best practices and guidelines that will help you to write more effectively for the Web.

Think about your audience

  • Identify and write for your audience
  • Tell your audience why the content is important to them
  • Think about what your audience already knows, then think about how to get them from there to what you need them to know
  • Ask yourself:
    • Who is my audience?
    • What do they already know about this subject?
    • What questions do they have?
    • What's the best outcome for my department?
      • What do I need to say to achieve this outcome?
    • What's the best outcome for my audience?
      • What do I need to say to achieve this outcome?

Make it easy to scan

  • Break your information into small paragraphs.
  • Use headings and subheadings to organize your content.
  • Keep your titles short (7 words or less) and include key words.
  • Use bulleted lists whenever possible.

Organize your content

  • Organize to meet your users' needs
  • Think through the questions your audience will ask and then organize your content in the order they'd ask them
  • Chronological organization
  • General first, then exceptions, conditions and other special information
  • Limit levels to three or four
  • Address separate audiences separately

Make it easy to find

  • Create unique content that users will find useful
  • Use keywords that users will use and are familiar with
  • Include keywords in both the body and title of the page

Address one person, not a group

  • Your content may affect millions of people, but you are only ever speaking to the one person reading it.
  • By addressing a single person, you address the reader directly.

Confusing plural

Clearer singular

The applicant must provide his or her mailing address and his or her identification number.

You must provide your mailing address and identification number.

Write short sections

  • Short sections break up content so it looks easier to understand
  • Gives you the opportunity to use informative headings and sub-headings
  • Use these headings to help you organize your content
  • Make sure that each is unique and meaningful, using words that your users understand and relate to the content

Avoid meaningless formal information

  • This type of content wastes user's time – get directly to your point!

examples:

  • Thinking outside the box
  • Value added
  • Best practice
  • For all intents and purposes
  • Touch base
  • Integrating quality solutions

Words matter

Verbs

  • Use an active voice
  • Use the simplest form of a verb
  • Avoid hidden verbs
  • Use "must" to indicate requirements
  • Use contractions when possible

Nouns and pronouns

  • Don't turn verbs into nouns
  • Use pronouns to speak directly to your reader
  • Minimize abbreviations but if you need to use some, make sure that the abbreviation is used in parenthesis following the full term at least once at the beginning of each page

Other word issues

  • Use short, simple words
  • Omit unnecessary words
  • Dealing with definitions
  • Be consistent
  • Avoid idioms
  • Avoid jargon
  • Avoid acronyms or abbreviations. If you need to use some, make sure that the abbreviation or acronyms are used in parenthesis following the full term at least once at the beginning of each page. Use Abbreviation at least once in parentheses for SeO)
  • Don't use slashes

Sentences

  • Write short sentences
  • Keep your subject, verb and object close together
  • Avoid double negatives and exceptions to exceptions
  • Place your main idea before exceptions and conditions
  • Place words carefully
  • Put conditions after the main clause
  • Put long conditions after the main clause

Other aids to clarity

  • Use examples
  • Use bulleted lists
    • Only use tables for data
    • Illustration of data or concept should only be use in last resort. Make sure your txt depict all concepts
  • emphasize important concepts
    • Avoid using ALL CAPS, or underlined text
  • Minimize cross-references
    • Most readers dislike them, and will just skip over them
  • Design your content for easy reading
    • Have five or six sections on each printed page (about two on each typewritten page)
    • Use lists often, but don't overuse them and don't have lists within lists
    • Use ragged right margins where possible, rather than fully justifying your text

Remember, your content is not clear unless your users can:

  • Find what they need
  • Understand what they find
  • Use what they find to meet their needs
